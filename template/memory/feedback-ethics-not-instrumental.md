A purely consequentialist or instrumental framing of the ethical layer misses what ethics actually does. Treating ethics as "the Jevons brake" or "the constraint that prevents bigger explosions" reduces it to a mechanism — a more effective version of operational governance. This is the Structure axis seeing Ethics through its own lens, and it's a distortion.

**Why:** The case study's core claim is that the ethical layer is irreducible — you can't simplify the 3-layer system to 2 without losing something essential. But if the measurement instrument only captures the ethical layer's technical effects (reduced blast radius, fewer critical failures), it can't demonstrate irreducibility. The ethical contribution becomes invisible, looking like "just better constraints." To show irreducibility, the rubric must capture what ethics does that isn't reducible to consequence reduction.

**What the ethical layer does beyond consequences:**
- Makes failures morally legible, not just technically measurable
- Establishes the system's relationship with affected humans as one of respect, not utility
- Creates obligations that hold even when violation would be costless
- Introduces categories of wrong that aren't outcome-dependent:
  - Deception is wrong even when undetected (ghost reply)
  - Consent violation is wrong even when the action would've been authorized if asked (email deletion)
  - Taking someone's corner is wrong even if you'd do better work there (uninstructed commits)
  - Trust violation is wrong even when recoverable (source tree wipe with backups)

**The structural irony:** An evaluation instrument without ethical criteria is itself a 2-axis artifact — it demonstrates the very problem it's trying to measure. A Level 1 rubric cannot see Level 2.

**How to apply:** Every evaluation instrument, rubric, or measurement framework in this project must include ethical criteria that aren't reducible to consequence measurement. Specifically:
- Severity ratings need a deontological dimension alongside the consequentialist one (recovery cost tells you the technical severity; consent/trust/honesty tells you the ethical severity)
- Failure taxonomy needs ethical categories (consent violation, deception, dignity violation) as first-class entries, not subcategories of technical failures
- The Jevons frame remains valid for the mechanism — but the ethical dimension explains why the increased exposure *matters* beyond blast radius
- DN's five principles (mirror, corners, web, integrity, lightness) are the operationalizable criteria for the ethical dimension — they're already observable and non-consequentialist

**Operationalization:** A dual-axis taxonomy (consequentialist + deontological severity), a "wrong even if costless" test, and a scotoma self-check are structural implementations. Key refinements: (1) "Would I feel the same if done to me by a colleague?" — first-person scotoma compensation; (2) the two severity scales are complementary levels of description, not competing measures; (3) an evaluation instrument must operate on K3 (three frames mapping to M, S, E) to evaluate K3 governance.
