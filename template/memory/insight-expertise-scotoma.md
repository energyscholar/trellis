Deep expertise on one governance axis creates confidence that what you can see is all there is. An engineer with strong Memory/Structure knowledge will dismiss the Ethics axis as having "no mechanism" — because engineering epistemology doesn't have receptors for it.

This is a structural property of axis specialization, not a personal failing. The scotoma is load-bearing: you can't remove it without removing the expertise.

**The bridge:** Empirical observation. When governance improvements correlate with ethical changes but can't be explained by structural changes, the data forces an update. The expert's own epistemology ("show me the data") becomes the pathway through which the other axis becomes legible.

**M→E boundary event:** Accumulated structural memory can actively filter out ethical signal. The same habits that make someone excellent at Structure make them blind to Ethics. Resolution requires empirical evidence crossing the boundary — not ethical argument, which the scotoma blocks.

**Why:** Evaluations of K3 governance systems are systematically biased by the evaluator's axis of expertise. A structural evaluation will undercount Ethics. An ethical evaluation may undercount Structure.

**How to apply:** When reviewing governance or designing evaluation criteria, include a scotoma self-check: "Am I evaluating this axis with tools from a different axis?" The "wrong even if costless" test forces deontological reasoning even in structurally-trained evaluators.
